# ACT-1090 — Deferred reactions, and an autoclose that finally stopped sweeping

## The pain that started it

Act could only make things happen because an event arrived. Every reaction in the framework fires off the back of a commit: something happened, the drain notices, a handler runs. There was no way to make something happen because *time passed*. The patterns that need that absence-of-an-event trigger are everywhere in real systems. If no payment lands within thirty minutes, expire the order. If a ticket goes quiet for a day, ping the customer. On the ninetieth day after resolution, archive the stream. None of these have an event to react to at the moment they need to fire, and Act had no primitive for them.

The one time-driven feature the framework did ship made the absence felt rather than filled. `.autocloses` ran a hardcoded background sweep: every twelve hours or so an `AutocloseController` woke up, paged through the entire store with `query_stats`, evaluated each state's predicate against every candidate stream, and truncated the matches in batches. It worked, but it was a single-purpose machine bolted to the side of the orchestrator. It had its own ticker, its own lifecycle wiring, its own cost model that scaled with total stream count rather than with how many streams were actually eligible, and its own latency floor of one whole cycle no matter how recently a stream qualified. Worse, it was the only thing in the framework that knew how to act on a schedule, and that knowledge was trapped inside autoclose where nothing else could reuse it.

## Why the obvious answer didn't fit

The design that RFC 0001 reached for looked clean on paper. The drain already revisits streams; a deferral is just letting a reaction set that next-visit time on purpose. No schedule entity, no cron, no new table — a timer is nothing more than a stream plus a reaction plus a future due-time. The RFC's load-bearing claim was that the persistence for that due-time was already in place: backoff's `next_attempt_at`, it assumed, was a column on the watermark that `claim()` already skipped on, so deferral could ride it for free with zero new `Store` surface.

That assumption was wrong, and Slice 1 disproved it the hard way. `next_attempt_at` lives only in process memory, on the `DrainController`. It is per-worker backoff pacing, and `claim()` has never read it. That is perfectly fine for a retry that needs to wait a few hundred milliseconds, but it falls apart the moment a deferral has to hold for thirty minutes across competing consumers. The lease expires after `leaseMillis` — on the order of ten seconds — and a different worker, with an empty in-memory backoff map, claims the stream and fires the reaction immediately, long before the deadline. An in-process due-time cannot survive a worker rotation, and a multi-worker deployment rotates workers constantly. Deferral, it turned out, *requires* persistence. The shape of the RFC held, but its premise about the mechanism did not, and the honest move was to amend the RFC in place rather than pretend the port stayed untouched.

Porting autoclose onto the new primitive surfaced three more wrong turns, each of which looked right until it ran. The first cut made the autoclose reaction fire on the aggregate's own stream — the natural choice, since that is where the events are. But the watermark and lease in Act are keyed by stream alone, so a deferral parks the *whole* stream, every reaction draining it included. An autoclose deferral on the aggregate would have quietly held back the aggregate's real reactions for the length of the cooldown. The second was subtler still: once autoclose became a reaction whose `source` is the aggregate, the close-cycle's own safety probe — which refuses to truncate a stream whose subscriptions lag the head — would see the autoclose subscription sitting behind and block the close it was itself requesting. A reaction that cannot close because it hasn't acked the event that told it to close is a deadlock with a straight face. The third was almost comic: a ninety-day cooldown expressed as a `setTimeout` delay overflows Node's 32-bit signed millisecond ceiling and fires immediately, turning a quarterly archive into a busy loop.

## The decision

Deferral is a persisted fact, not an in-process hint. A new required `Store.defer(input, deferred_at)` method joins the watermark verb family — claim, ack, block, defer — and stamps a `deferred_at` time onto each matched stream's row. `claim()` skips any stream whose `deferred_at` is still in the future, which is the whole correctness story: because the skip lives in the shared store, every competing worker honors the same deferral, and because a deferred stream is never claimed, its `retry` counter is structurally untouched. A defer is not a failure. `ack`, `block`, `reset`, and `unblock` all clear `deferred_at`, because each of those means the stream has either made progress or been taken in hand. The in-process timer survives, but demoted to what it always should have been: a local promptness optimization. `DeferTimer` in `libs/act/src/internal/defer-timer.ts` holds a small `stream → due-time` map and one collapsed wake timer so the deferring worker re-arms itself at the due moment instead of waiting for the next poll, and it clamps long horizons to the 32-bit ceiling and re-arms across the gap, since the persisted column is what actually gates the re-claim. Correctness no longer depends on the timer at all.

The reaction expresses both deferral and close by throwing. `DeferSignal` (`defer-signal.ts`) and `CloseSignal` (`close-signal.ts`) ride the dispatcher's existing `try/catch` the same way `NonRetryableError` does, so the handler loop needs no new return channel. A defer carries the wall-clock time to revisit; the drain turns it into a `HandleResult.defer`, holds the triggering event pending without advancing the watermark, persists the due-time, and re-delivers when it comes due. A close carries the stream to retire, an optional archiver, and the watermark to ack first; `build_handle` turns it into a `HandleResult.close`, the drain collects the target, and the `DrainController` hands it to the orchestrator's `on_close`, which runs the very same `run_close_cycle` machinery `app.close` uses. Closing stays an orchestrator capability — reactions only signal the intent, so the public reaction-scoped surface gains nothing.

Autoclose is now a synthesized reaction rather than a sweep. At construction, before `classify_registry` runs, the orchestrator (`libs/act/src/act.ts`) walks every state that declared `.autocloses(policy)` and injects one reaction registered against every event that state owns. It runs on a *synthetic per-aggregate stream*, `__autoclose__:<stream>`, with the aggregate as its source — and that synthetic target is the answer to the first wrong turn, because keeping the autoclose lease off the aggregate's own watermark means its deferrals can never hold back the aggregate's real reactions. The handler reads the aggregate's live head via `query_stats` (so a reopened stream re-evaluates correctly), and if the declarative policy matches it throws `CloseSignal`, carrying `at: head.id` so the close-cycle safety probe sees the autoclose subscription caught up rather than lagging — the answer to the second wrong turn. If the policy has not matched but carries an `after` cooldown, it defers to `head.created + the window`. If it has no time gate, it acks and waits for the next event. The off-hours `autocloseWindow` gate is preserved: outside the window the handler defers to the next cycle instead of closing. The close is still automatic and zero-touch, exactly as before.

One thing was deliberately taken away. `.autocloses` no longer accepts a function predicate. An opaque `(stream, head, count) => boolean` has no derivable due-time to defer to and no terminal event to react against, so it cannot be compiled into the reaction that replaced the sweep. The declarative `{ is, after, reaches, or }` policy object is the whole surface now, and the function form throws at build with a migration message. Anything the policy can't express — different cooldowns per terminal event, per-stream metadata, a saga waiting on the absence of an event — moves into the host's own logic calling `app.close`, which is exactly where arbitrary conditions belong.

## What this teaches

The recurring lesson is that "we already persist that" deserves to be checked before it becomes load-bearing. The RFC's elegance rested on a column that didn't exist, and the elegance survived the correction precisely because the *shape* of the idea — scheduling as a stream's next-visit time — was independent of where the due-time lived. When a design's premise turns out false, the question is whether the premise was structural or incidental. Here it was incidental, so the fix was one new port method, not a redesign.

The deeper lesson is about reuse through subtraction. Autoclose had accreted a controller, a ticker, a paging loop, and a cost model, all to do one thing on a timer. Once the framework grew a real deferral primitive, almost all of that machinery turned out to be a special case of "a reaction that defers and then closes." Deleting `run_autoclose_cycle` and `AutocloseController` and re-expressing autoclose as a synthesized reaction made it smaller, more correct (it now reacts to the live head, so reopened streams are handled for free), and — this is the point — it made the scheduling capability general. The thing that used to be trapped inside autoclose is now the drain's, and the public timer surface tracked in #1049 will be built on the same `defer` outcome rather than on a second bespoke sweep.
