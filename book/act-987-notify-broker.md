# The wakeup is not the truth

Every event-sourced system eventually rediscovers the same distinction: there is the log, which must never lie, and there is the tap on the shoulder that says go look at the log. Act draws this line explicitly. Correctness lives in claim and drain over the durable store; the notify channel is documented as a hint, and the framework is built to survive that hint never arriving. Postgres LISTEN and NOTIFY made a fine first tap on the shoulder, until you count connections. Every worker that wants low-latency wakeups holds a dedicated listener connection, and managed Postgres meters those out like a landlord meters hot water.

Ticket 987 asked for the obvious move: keep the log where it is and move the tap to something built for fanning out. The shape of the answer was decided by a sentence already sitting in the port documentation. Because notify is a hint and not a contract, the wakeup channel is swappable without touching a single durability guarantee. The whole package is that sentence made executable. A decorator wraps the durable adapter, delegates every store method through a proxy that rebinds methods so private state stays home, and overrides exactly two behaviors: after a successful commit it publishes a small message to a broker, and when the orchestrator asks to subscribe it listens to that broker instead of the database. Each wrapped instance mints an origin id and drops its own messages on receipt, which is the port's self-filtering contract handled once, in the decorator, instead of once per broker.

The discipline of the repo did most of the design work. The broker contract is two methods, publish and subscribe, with fan-out semantics stated in the doc-comment because a queue that delivers to one consumer would starve every other worker's wakeup. Redis pub/sub fits that contract naturally and shipped implemented, taking connected clients as arguments rather than depending on any redis package, in the same sink-injection style the http helpers use. Kafka shipped as a scaffold that refuses loudly, and the refusal is the interesting part. Wiring a producer and consumer is an afternoon; the semantics are not. Consumer groups deliver each message to one member per group, the opposite of fan-out, so a correct adapter needs an ephemeral group per process, latest-offset semantics because a stale wakeup is worthless, and an answer for rebalance pauses that silently delay delivery. Guessing at those against no real deployment would have produced the worst kind of adapter, one that works in the demo and starves workers in production. The scaffold throws with guidance instead.

The proof obligations came straight from the audit that filed the ticket. The decorator passes the full store TCK wrapping a real Postgres adapter, lease semantics included, which is the executable way of saying the proxy adds nothing and removes nothing from the durable contract. A chaos test hands the decorator a broker that drops every second message, duplicates every third, and delays every fifth, then asserts that drain processes exactly what it processes over a bare store. And an end-to-end test commits through one wrapped instance and watches a full orchestrator on another instance wake and drain through the broker alone, no manual pipeline calls, because the tap on the shoulder is the product and the test should prove the tap works. The lesson worth keeping is older than the package: when a system separates truth from urgency cleanly enough, urgency becomes a commodity you can buy from whatever infrastructure happens to be cheap that year.
